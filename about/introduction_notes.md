    # LQ
    
    ### Learning the Quran. Loving the Quran. Living the Quran.
    
    Access to the Quran. For the common human.
    
    ## Background
    
    When I was younger, I was not a very curious child. At least, I don't think so. I didn't have many questions, and I was mostly happy to accept things as they were and lived my life. I noticed certain patterns, the way some things worked, and I accepted them as "this is the way things are", and moved on.
    
    I was not the smartest child, as you can probably tell.
    
    Sometimes when patterns were broken, that kinda tripped a switch in my mind. And I would think almost obsessively about the broken pattern. And the questions it raised. I guess that's when my curiousity got triggered.
    
    One of the patterns I noticed was in almost every realm of knowledge, whether it's physics, chemistry, biology, or pyschology, anthropology, sociology, or politics, arts, or culture, there was generally growth over time. People didn't start from zero. They added knowledge to existing knowledge bases. They stood on the shoulders of people who came before them, and expanded the existing knowledge. And this was generally positive thing.
    
    However, the realm of knowledge that broke the pattern there was simply how to be human. Every human being had to learn about being human from scratch. Sure, we took in information from outside. But, at the end of the day, there is no consistent knowledge base, that tells you how to be a human. You have to discover that on your own. Of course there are individual books, with people own experiences about things in their own lives. But those seemed to be very specific in 2 ways. First, it was very specific to the person who wrote it (because it was their experience, obviously). Second, it was about a specific topic, that was disconnected from the larger experience of being human.
    
    But, in other realms of knowledge, there seem to be these core knowledge trees (which may branch off into more fringe topics), and those knowledge bases grow consistenly with every generation. We didn't have something like that for just being human.
    
    So, what I decided was that I would write that book. I would write my own experiences, and start that core knowledge tree. And other people could add their own observations and experiences, and grow the knowledge base. That way, for future generations, they wouldn't have to figure out some basic truths about the human experience that their forefathers had already figured out. They would accept those things as basic truths, and go on to expand the frontiers of the human experience.
    
    As I got older, I made a key realization my plan that, in hindsight, seems very obvious.
    
    There is one key difference within the realm of knowledge that is the human experience, and every other realm of knowledge, whether it's the arts, the physical sciences, the social sciences or anything else. Unlike any other realm of knowledge, the important truths and lessons and takeaways about being human, they have to be experienced to be truly understood, and accepted. You can learn 1 + 1 = 2 in math and science. But within the human experience, sometimes 1 + 1 can be 11. And unless you have experienced that, it's not easy to understand. I mean, we can use words to describe how 1 + 1 might be 11, but to truly understand it, the only option is experience. So, knowledge of the human experience is different than knowledge of physics, psychology, or politics. The knowledge of the human experience is life, it's alive. It happens as time goes on, as you experience things. You can't simply learn about it in a book. Comparitively, the other knowledge areas could almost be described as dead, inanimate, static. Knowledge of the human experience is alive, dynamic, and changing.
    
    On top that, the implication of knowledge that has to be experienced is that the knowldge will be unique to the person experiencing it, based on at least 2 aspects. The same experience could cause 2 different people to learn 2 different but equally valid lessons, based on 2 things, their personality, and the time they undergo the exeperience. After all, no man (or woman) steps in the same river (of experience) twice, for it is not the same river, and he (or she) is not the same man (or woman).
    
    Of course, this made me reconsider my idea entirely. A book by its nature is only going to contain static knowledge. Given that static nature, the only possible option is to write that knowledge in such a flexible, adaptable way such that the knowledge would be applicable to experiences across a multitude of lives, times, and perspectives. On top of that, even if the core body of knowledge is available, it doesn't mean much if a person has to learn it all over again from scratch anyway.
    
    But still, I could not let go of the core idea. Because there are some things that are universal across the human experience. Certain things, we all have to deal with. Ideas like good and evil, justice and mercy. Questions of meaning and purpose. Experiences like family, society and community. Those things, for the human experience, are as fundamental and universal as 1 + 1 = 2.
    
    So in theory, we should be able to build a core knowledge base across those universalities, and then grow from there. But, the fact remained that all meaningful knowledge in the realm of the human experience would still have to be, y'know, experienced. And you could not move forward, or grow, if you had to take the time to relearn (read: experience) the basics.
    
    Time passed.
    
    I got older. I learned about life, and about the Quran. And I got married.
    
    It was one winter evening, when I was sitting in a cafe with my wife, talking to her about a perspective on the miraculous nature of the Quran. And my beloved wife said something that connected several dots in my mind.
    
    I described what I felt as a miracle of the Quran. And my wife's reply was "Isn't that kind of like your idea of a book on humanity?"
    
    That simple question connected a bunch of previously disparate, half-baked ideas and thoughts into what you see before you now.
    
    ## Vision
    
    I think we, us, human beings, living this life, this is an incredible experience. I mean, when you come down to the bare bones, we’re all something inexplicable, piloting meat suits with bones, on a spinning piece of rock and water, rotating around a glowing ball of fire. 
    
    And even that wild sentence barely expresses the incredulity of the human experience.
    
    So, I think it’s safe to say that the human experience is an incredible thing. It is a self-evident truth.
    
    The other truth that’s as axiomatic, is that none of us have any idea what we’re doing. We’re all building the road as we drive, figuring out the experience as we experience it.
    
    And this incredible, wild experience is often bad for the vast majority of us, the vast majority of the humans experiencing it.
    
    Everyone has their own ways of improving this experience. In fact, from a certain perspective, that’s all everyone really is trying to do. Improve their own experience of life. We’re all trying to experience a better today than yesterday.
    
    This idea, along with the previously disconnected idea of creating a knowledge base about the human experience (along with a few other ideas), came together to 
    
    In my experience, the best way to figure out and improve our lives is with the Quran. The best knowledge base that complements and describes the human experience, with the restrictions of learning about the human experience, that is the Quran. 
    
    And this is my attempt to share what I have learned.
    
    In brief, the proposal is to build and maitain a relationship with the Quran so that the Quran becomes a commentary on our experiences. Ideally, what we build up to is a sustained connection of knowing what the Quran says, such that when we go through an important experience in life, we automatically recall a relevant part of the Quran that provides a commentary on the experience.
    
    This is what I call **Living with the Quran**.
    
    ## Living with the Quran
    
    The idea of living with the Quran refers to living our day to day lives within the view of the Quran.
    
    What does that really mean though?
    
    Well, so when we are living our normal, every day lives, there are many things that are directly and explicitly, and implicitly and indirectly, guiding, shaping, and forming our thoughts. It's not just things we explicitly learned, like manners, or social rules, etc. There are also things that are deeper than that, like attitudes and habits you absored from your friends, whether you view the world as a happy place, or a sad place, whether you yourself are a happy, or sad person, or things we absorbed when we were children and didn't think to question.
    
    When I say living with the Quran, I mean to enhance all those things with the view presented by the Quran. It's more than just a rules of halal and haram. It's more than rules of prayer, and worship, even more than Aqeedah, or our creed of God, the Prophet, and the Quran.
    
    All those things tell you how to live. But those things are not actual living.
    
    It's the difference between someone describing how a cake tastes, and eating a slice of cake yourself. You can have all the information about the cake, how to eat, what to do before, during, and after eating. But all that information is different than actually eating the cake.
    
    That's the difference I'm talking about.
    
    I think we repeat how to live a lot. All ulamah, fuqaha, any mufti you find, any lecture you pick, they will teach you how to live, how to pray, what to do and what not to do. And, rightfully so. All those things are required. Before we actually live, we need to know how.
    
    But that does not replace the actual living.
    
    And, that, actual living, well, that's something we all do on our own. We all live our own separate lives. All the ulamah, fuqaha, asatizah in the world cannot live your life for you (they've got their own lives to live).
    
    So, when I say "living with the Quran" what I'm talking about is your relationship with the Quran, when you're actually living.
    
    And that relationship can be so vast. It's not just a friendship. It's not just guidance. It's not just mentorship. It's not just loving care. It's not just encouragement. It's all those things, and so much more.
    
    What it is, is actually up to you. Just like you will live your own life, and no one will live it for you, you will form your own relationship with the Quran. No one else will form it for you.
    
    The Quran is not just a book of guidance, it's not just a book of do's and don't's. It's so much more.  It's a friend, a light, a guidance, a companion. It should make you happy like nothing else in the world (actually the words of the Quran).
    
    And, I think when we begin living with the Quran, we can begin to appreciate it in our own way. And like I said before, that's something no scholar will be able to do for you.
    
    The rest of this post will try to present some ways in which we can begin living with the Quran.
    
    But before, we get to that, let's address some preliminary questions.
    
    ## Why am I doing this?
    
    That's a very good question. Not just cause you don't know me, but, I mean my whole idea is that you need to find your own relationship with the Quran. So, if I'm talking about something that you have to do on your own anyway, what am I actually doing, and why am I doing it.
    
    About the first part, being who am I to tell you this stuff, honestly, that's the main reason I didn't try to do this earlier. There were 2 questions that I couldn't answer in my mind.
    
    Firstly, I'm not a scholar, I'm not an aalim. I'm just a person like you. So why would you listen to me?
    
    Secondly, I mean, we've all heard that example of "you wouldn't go for medicine to a random person, so why would you go for the Quran to a random person?"
    
    And yeah, those are valid questions. You don't need to listen to me in the same way that you need to listen to a scholar. I'm not telling you how to live. That's the job for the scholars. But, that's okay, cause I'm not telling you something a scholar would tell you. I'm just telling you about my life. And I'm hoping that, my experience might help other people in their own relationship with the Quran.
    
    I'm not telling you about halal and haram. I'm not telling the right way to pray. I'm not telling you the rulings on lipstick, nail polish, mortgages and car loans.
    
    I'm going to tell you what I learend from the Quran, and I'm hoping that will help you. (And if you think that only a scholar can tell you about the Quran, cause all the Quran tells you is what to do and what not to do.. then I would beg you to at least give this a listen).
    
    About the second question, that you wouldn't get medicine from a random person, so why would you get Quran from a random person? Honestly, that's part of what I mean. For a long time, I tried to answer that question, and I had no answer. Until recently.
    
    What I realized was that the question is not fair. It's correct that you shouldn't get medicine from random people. I should go to the doctor. But I don't need to go to the doctor for tylenol. I don't need to go to the doctor for Buckleys, for Band-aids, for cough medicine. Not just that, but taking medicine is a critical part of taking care of your health overall. You don't go to the doctor cause you like the medicine. You go to the doctor, cause you need the medicine, to improve your health. And again, taking medicine is a critical part of taking care of your health, but it's certainly not the only thing. Taking care of your health includes your diet, your exercise, your mental health. And you don't need a doctor for all that information.
    
    Taking medicine for your health is like learning the halal and the haram, the do's and the don't's, for the Quran. It's critical.
    
    But it's certainly not the whole thing. Medicine is a part of a larger whole. It is a critical part. And you need the doctor for it. But it is by no means the whole of taking care of your health.
    
    Similarly, learning the Quran is not just learning the halal and the haram. Learning the halal and the haram is a part of a larger whole. It is a critical part. And you absolutely need scholars for it. But it is by no means the whole of your relationship with the Quran.
    
    Not only that, if you reduce taking care of your health to just taking medicine, without caring about diet, or exercise, or mental health, well I mean you'll survive. But I promise you, it will be completely different life than if you took care of your health as a whole.
    
    Similarly, if you reduce your relationship with the Quran to simply learning the halal and the haram, then, I mean you'll survive. But I promise you, oh I promise you, life with the Quran as a whole... well, it's completely different.
