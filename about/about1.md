# Living with the Quran

## Description

This is a post that discusses the idea I call "living with the Quran".

## What is "living with the Quran"?

The idea of living with the Quran refers to living our day to day lives within the view of the Quran.

What does that really mean though?

Well, so when we are living our normal, every day lives, there are many things that are directly and explicitly, and implicitly and indirectly, guiding, shaping, and forming our thoughts. It's not just things we explicitly learned, like manners, or social rules, etc. There are also things that are deeper than that, like attitudes and habits you absored from your friends, whether you view the world as a happy place, or a sad place, whether you yourself are a happy, or sad person, or things we absorbed when we were children and didn't think to question.

When I say living with the Quran, I mean to enhance all those things with the view presented by the Quran. It's more than just a rules of halal and haram. It's more than rules of prayer, and worship, even more than Aqeedah, or our creed of God, the Prophet, and the Quran.

All those things tell you how to live. But those things are not actual living.

It's the difference between someone describing how a cake tastes, and eating a slice of cake yourself. You can have all the information about the cake, how to eat, what to do before, during, and after eating. But all that information is different than actually eating the cake.

That's the difference I'm talking about.

I think we repeat how to live a lot. All ulamah, fuqaha, any mufti you find, any lecture you pick, they will teach you how to live, how to pray, what to do and what not to do. And, rightfully so. All those things are required. Before we actually live, we need to know how.

But that does not replace the actual living.

And, that, actual living, well, that's something we all do on our own. We all live our own separate lives. All the ulamah, fuqaha, asatizah in the world cannot live your life for you (they've got their own lives to live).

So, when I say "living with the Quran" what I'm talking about is your relationship with the Quran, when you're actually living.

And that relationship can be so vast. It's not just a friendship. It's not just guidance. It's not just mentorship. It's not just loving care. It's not just encouragement. It's all those things, and so much more.

What it is, is actually up to you. Just like you will live your own life, and no one will live it for you, you will form your own relationship with the Quran. No one else will form it for you.

The Quran is not just a book of guidance, it's not just a book of do's and don't's. It's so much more.  It's a friend, a light, a guidance, a companion. It should make you happy like nothing else in the world (actually the words of the Quran).

And, I think when we begin living with the Quran, we can begin to appreciate it in our own way. And like I said before, that's something no scholar will be able to do for you.

The rest of this post will try to present some ways in which we can begin living with the Quran.

But before, we get to that, let's address some preliminary questions.

Honestly? Cause the Quran, the word of God, the Light brought by the Prophet, deserves as many people who can appreciate it deeper as possible. It's not a question of you or me. The word of God is in the highest place possible. It is already Noble. It is already an act of loving care by God. Nothing we do or do not do will take any of those things away.

But its miracle deserves to be appreciated. And if I can help spread that appreciation, well "And as for The Blessing of your Master, then talk about it".

## What about guidance? If I go to the Quran unprepared, am I going to get misguided?

That's another common question or concern people have. God says that with the Quran, many are guided, and with the Quran, many are misguided.

This is true. In fact, an argument can be made that any deviant Muslim sect was misguided based on the wrong understanding of the Quran.

But does that mean that we don't try to understand the Quran at all?

I mean, in theory, any time you drive, you can get in an accident. Does that mean you don't drive at all? Any time you go to work, you can make a mistake and get fired. Does that mean you don't go to work? Anything worth doing comes with advantages and risks, pros and cons. That doesn't mean you don't take risks. It just means that you prepare yourself, before you take the risk.

And yes, the argument can be made that all those other things will only have wordly consequences. And misunderstanding the Quran will have consequences in the hereafter.

But, how are we so certain that not understanding the Quran at all will not have consequences in the hereafter?

Things like this can be argued for eternity. But, let's take a look at what the Quran says.

About guidance, the Quran does say "God guides whoever He wills." And the Quran does say "He misguides with it a lot [of people], and He guides with it a lot [of people]". 

But, the Quran goes on to say "And no, no one is misguided with it except the inherently corrupt."

The Quran also says "God misguides whoever He wants, and guides whoever wants". The Quran also says "Whosoever God guided, then he is the one committed to guidance." The Quran says that it is a guidance for the Cautious. It is a guidance, an act of loving care from God, for the Muslims.

So, if you ask me, it sounds like the Quran itself is giving you the answer for who gets misguided, and who gets guided.

If you agree, then there is basically one remaining question.

Who are the inherently corrupt, with regards to the Quran? How do you become the inherently corrupt regarding the Quran?

I think the Quran itself answers those questions as well.

We will talk about this in more details in the subsequent section. But, in one place, the Quran clarifies that it is a guidance for the Cautious, for those who submit. And previously, it mentioned that no one gets misguided from it except the inherently corrupt. Putting the two together, it sounds like as long as you will be cautious, and you will submit to what it says, you can, at the very least, avoid being inherently corrupt, and thereby avoid misguidance.

Of course, I'm not an authority on guidance. I do not, I cannot, guarantee I know the right way for guidance. So, if you still don't feel comfortable, and you think another approach is better and more cautious, then you should follow your heart.

But if you do agree, or at least are willing to explore, and you are willing to come to the Quran for guidance because you are cautious, and you are willing to submit to what it says, then, let's crack on!

## How do we talk about it?

For any important thing in life, what can be equally important (and arguably more important) is how you talk about the thing. How do you approach the topic? Because the thing is, for human beings, when we talk about something, anything, when it comes down to it, we're not actually talking about the thing itself. If we're being exact, what we're talking about it, is our experience of the thing, our perception of it.

It's an easy thing to forget. Because, generally speaking, it goes without saying. But when we talk about important things, we have to keep that in mind. And, the more impotant the topic is, the more important it is to discuss its perception. And, the Quran is arguably the most important of topics.

So, to talk about the Quran, first we must talk about how to talk about it. How to approach it. What our perception of the Quran should be.

And in our case, with the Quran, since the Quran has otherwordly importance as well, importance for our hereafter as well, our perception, our approach to the Quran is doubly as important.

As I mentioned above, from my understanding, whether we get guidance or not from the Quran may actually depend on our approach to the Quran. If we approach it thinking we know better, well that's not a good sign. If we come to the Quran looking for validation of our opinions, that's not a good thing. If we approach the Quran looking to use it as an argument to beat down someone else, that's definitely not a good thing. It can be argued that the historically, we were taught that the best approach to the Quran for normal, every day people, is to not approach it at all.

So, how do we talk about the Quran? How do we perceive it? How do we approach it?

Well, I mean let's see how the Quran shows us how to approach it.

After the preface, which is Surah Fatiha, which we can discuss at another time, the Quran starts with "alif-laam-meem".

Does anyone know what it means? Can we know what it means?

Historically speaking, we don't know what it means. Only God knows the true meaning of "alif-laam-meem". That begins our approach to the Quran. Alif-laam-meem tells us 2 things.

First, it tells you that you don't know everything. That is the beginning our approach to the Quran. Many wise people have said things along the lines of that idea, that realizing your ignorance is the beginning of knowledge. And the Quran starts us off with that. It tells you that, almost literally, no matter how learned you are or will be, you don't know the first thing about it. The implication of that is that you have to realize that you're coming to it to learn, for the Quran to tell you. You're not coming to it because you think you know everything. Because, if you think you know everything, then alif-laam-meem.

Secondly, it tells you that you WON'T know everything. You can be the greatest Quran scholar in the world, and you still will not be able to say that you know what it means for sure. So, not only does it tell you how to start with the Quran, it tells you how to continue with the Quran. You learn about the Quran, and you learn humility. You learn to keep asking God for guidance. You learn that you're on a journey, and you DON'T know how you will end. Because you know for sure you still don't know everything.

So, that's the first part of our approach to the Quran.

Next, the Quran goes on to say something along the lines of "That is the book, no doubt in it is a guidance for the Cautious."

That gives us the second part of our approach to the Quran. I'm using a liberal translation of the word, but I'm hoping it gets the idea across. The Quran from the beginning calls itself guidance for the Cautious. It can be many things to many people, and in fact, it will go on to describe that a bit. But it first identifies itself as a guidance for the Cautious. To explain what I understood from it, let me give some background first.

Now, I'm simplifying here quite a lot, but as a general rule, when something, anything, happens in life, anything at all, there are 2 ways to approach it, react to it, or think about it.

Think about anything in life. You can approach it in 2 ways. First way, let's call it the simple way. You approach the thing, you react to it, without thinking about it beforehand, without looking at or analyzing it, without any sort of caution. You see something shiny, you go to it. You touch it. You taste it. Whatever. The point is, you approach it, you react to it without any thought. Almost like a baby, or a child.

The second way, well, the other way is you think about it beforehand. You have some sort of a thought process of what the thing means, what it implies, what it can do, what might happen after, and then you approach it, and then you react to it. Let's say you feel anger at something. You think about it before react. You're walking in a dark alley and you hear a noise, you think about it before you go see it. You see a candy bar on the ground, you think about it before you eat it. The simple way to say it, you show a little bit of caution before you react.

For example, take 2 people. They both woke up, got ready for work, and they're about to leave. The first person just opens the door, and leaves, not a single thought about what the weather outside might be like. The second person takes a quick look out of the window, sees that it's raining, and grabs an umbrella before he leaves. At least concerning the weather, the second person showed a bit of caution, while the first person did not.

Between the two approaches, I hope it's clear what the more mature, more intelligent, arguably better approach is.

Before you react to a thing, ideally you think about what it might mean first. You show a bit of caution.

It seems like that is what the Quran might be talking about. Being Cautious. However, there's something interesting here.

The Quran does not mention what the people are cautious about. It says that it is a guidance for the Cautious, but it does not say what those people are cautious about.

Sometimes, we will see that the Quran does mention what we should be cautious about. Sometimes, it's about displeasing God, or the Hellfire, or the relationships of the womb. Sometimes it is specific. But here, it does not specify what we should be cautious about.

Generally speaking, as a rule in the Quran (which we will learn later), when the Quran leaves something open-ended, we don't restrict it.

So, we can say that the Quran is talking about people who are cautious in general. They're not cautious about one specific thing or another, but as a general rule, they approach life in a cautious manner. Anything that they come across in life, they are careful with it. They treat it cautiously. (And we will learn later, about the other type of people, the impulsive types, those who, as a general rule, don't think things through before they react, those who "explode" on to whatever is in front of them, and the Quran does not have good things to say about them).

So, this is the second part of our approach to the Quran. To get guidance from the Quran, we have to approach it, and life itself, cautiously. As a general rule, we are not reckless about life.

So, to summarize how do we talk about the Quran, so far, we've learned two things about it.

First, we talk about it realizing our own ignorance. We talk about it knowing that we don't know everything. That, we are here to learn from the Quran, not impose our opinions on it. 
Second, we approach the Quran, and life in general, cautiously. We are careful about it. We take it seriously. We aren't reckless about it. We protect ourselves from problems that might come from mistreating it.

The Quran will go on to tell us more about how we should approach it. It will tell us other qualities of people who will be guided from it, who will be friends of it, who will be cared for by it. It will tell us about others as well, those will abuse it, mistreat it, become lost from it. But for now, we start with those 2 things.

We are not coming to the Quran to override what it says with our opinion. We're not coming to the Quran to override the Aqeedah that the Muslim ummah has had for centuries. We're not coming to the Quran so that it can validate what we already think.

We are coming to the Quran as a beggar at the door of God. We are coming to the Quran because we are cautious about how we want to live life. We are coming to the Quran because we are willing to accept what it tells us, without overriding it with our own opinion.

So, keeping those 2 things in mind, let's talk about it. Let's talk about living with the Quran.

And in this case as well, the why is the most important.

There are two approaches to the answer. The first answer, I will go over it quickly, even though it is the more important. Why should we live with the Quran? Because we need to have a real relationship with the Quran.

I will not go into why we need to have a relationship with the Quran. I'm assuming that is already important to us, which is why we're here. Why should we have a relationship with the Quran, why should it be important to us, that is a different topic. Right now, we're talking about the how. Now, the second answer to the why question, although it's not as important as the first, it might be more relevant to our discussion now.

In my experience, one of the ways you will experience a miracle of the Quran for yourself is by having a real, day to day relationship with it. What do I mean by that?

So, the Quran has many miracles. I'm sure we all believe that as a matter of our Faith. Out of the many miracles of the Quran, there is a special miracle that occurs when you begin to have a real relationship with the Quran in your daily life, when you begin to live with it.

Although it's not called out explicitly as a long term pattern, it was described by the companion Hadhrat Omar RA at the passing of the Prophet. During that time, when his life was shaken in a way we cannot even begin to imagine, Hadhrat Abu Bakr RA reminded him of an Ayah of the Quran, and Hadhrat Omar said that it seemed like it was the first time he was hearing that Ayah.

I think that is a miracle that we begin to experience for ourselves when we begin to live with the Quran. You will go through things in life. And you will need help. And you will need guidane. And you will need a friend. And you will need encouragement. And you will find the Quran there, responding to you in the moment, as if it was sent directly to you, as if it's the first time you hear that Ayah. It may not be a miracle for anyone else, because no one else will have had an experience like you did. No one else will be going through what you're going through. So, when the Quran shows up for you as a miracle in that moment, others may not be able to relate. But I promise you, you will understand.

It is a special type of miracle because it is very personal to an individual, just as personal as their relationship to the Quran.

It is also special because it will keep the miracle of the Quran alive for you. You will come back to the same Ayaat you've heard time and time again, but you will come to them with a new appreciation. Although the words of the Quran will not change, you would have changed as you experience life, and the unchanging, timeless words of the Quran will come to you in a new way.

Having a real, day to day relationship with the Quran, living with the Quran, keeps the Quran alive in your life. Living with the Quran changes the Quran for you, from a book you read, to a friend, a teacher, a mentor, a loving companion, a guide, and a warner.

That is the second reason why we should live with the Quran.

Actually, as an indirect part of living with the Quran, we will also experience another timeless miracle of the Quran. But we will describe that in a later section.

## How do we live with the Quran?

With respect to our current topic, this is arguably the most important question. How do we have a relationship with the Quran? How do we start living with it?

Well, the way I've seen it, I think there are 3 stages.

See, our goal is to have a real relationship with the Quran, where it is a significant, impactful part of our daily lives. So, our goal is to live with the Quran.

But how?

How do we live with the Quran?

Well, how do we live with anything? How do we keep anything in our mind in our day to day lives?

From what I can tell, there are 2 ways things are involved or repeated in our day to day lives. First is if it's something external to us. So, take work. Or school. Or chores. All of those things will come up in your day to day life, regardless of you doing something about it.

Second way something comes up in our day to day life is if it's important to us. If we treat something with significance, with importance, with love, then we think about it constantly. It comes up constantly in our minds, regardless of whatever else we might be doing in our day to day lives.

And, here, since we're talking about our personal relationship with the Quran, then by definition, it can't be something external. So the only way really that we will live with the Quran, is if we learn to love the Quran, and we learn to treat it with significance, and with importance.

So, our main goal is to live with the Quran, and to do that, we must learn to love the Quran.

How do we learn to love the Quran?

Well, how do we learn to love anything?

As far as I can tell, love for something, real love, begins with the knowledge of the beloved. You don't, in fact you can't, love something you don't know. You can claim love for it. But if you don't take the time to learn about it, then you don't love the thing for what it is (because you don't know what it is). You love it for what you think it is. So, reallly, if you love something you don't know, you just love what you have told yourself.

So, going to back our goals.

Our main goal is to live with the Quran.

To do that, our goal is to love the Quran.

Now, to do that, our goal must be to know the Quran.

And, that, that is where we begin. We learn about the Quran, and we will fall in love with it. And we fall in love with it, we will keep thinking about it in our day to day lives, and we will live with it.

And, when we begin to learn about the Quran, we will experience another timeless miracle, as I mentioned earlier. The Quran has many miracles, some we know about, and others we don't know about. No one can claim to encompass all the miracles of the Quran.

However, what was its initial miracle. What was the miracle that convinced even the disbelieving Arabs that the Quran cannot be human? After all, if they're accusing the Prophet of magic, it means that he is doing something that is not normal, not human.

What was that thing? It can't be the scientific knowledge in the Quran, nor the historical knowledge, because they were not a learned people. If they did not have extra knowledge, how did they judge the Quran to be magic, to be beyond human, a miracle?

The only thing it could have been was the words of the Quran itself, its language.

And, as we learn about the Quran, we will dive deeper into its language, and we will hope to see the same magic, the same miracle, that those bedouin Arabs saw, that convinced them that the Quran has to be something beyond human.
